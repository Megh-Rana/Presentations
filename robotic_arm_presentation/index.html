Project Overview

What We Built

A 4-DOF robotic arm with a gripper, driven by four servo motors and controlled wirelessly over Bluetooth from an Android app we built ourselves using MIT App Inventor.

The Brain

An ESP32-WROOM-32 microcontroller handles both the Bluetooth Classic SPP stack and PWM signal generation for all four servos simultaneously — no additional PWM driver IC needed.

📡
The Android app sends single-character prefixed angle commands (A090, B045…) over Bluetooth. The ESP32 parses and drives the corresponding servo in real time.

03

Power Supply Design

Supply Chain

Two 3.7 V 18650 Li-Ion cells in series give a nominal 7.4 V (up to ~8.4 V fully charged). This feeds the 7805 linear regulator which outputs a clean 5 V for the servo rail.

The ESP32 dev board has its own onboard 3.3 V LDO, so it is powered separately from the USB or the same 5 V rail via its VIN pin.

Why 7805?

The LM7805 (TO-220) is simple, robust, and handles the ~1 A peak current demand of four servos moving simultaneously. The TO-220 package dissipates heat efficiently without a heatsink at our load levels.

04

ESP32 & GPIO Pin Assignment

Why These Pins?

The ESP32Servo library uses hardware timers (LEDC peripheral) for PWM. Pins 23, 27, 18, and 13 are all output-capable, PWM-compatible GPIO pins with no conflicts with the Bluetooth stack, SPI flash, or strapping pins.

PWM Parameters

Each servo is attached with a pulse range of 500 µs – 2400 µs, covering the full 0°–180° range of the SG90/MG90. The ESP32 allocates one hardware timer per servo (timers 0–3).

Software & App

ESP32 Firmware

Written in Arduino C++ using the ESP32Servo library for hardware-timer PWM and the built-in BluetoothSerial library for Classic BT SPP. The firmware reads incoming characters, buffers them until a new command prefix arrives, then parses and drives the servo.

1
Allocate 4 hardware PWM timers on boot
2
Attach each servo with 500–2400 µs pulse range
3
Start Bluetooth as "RobotArm" device
4
Loop: read BT stream → parse command → servo.write(angle)

Android App — MIT App Inventor

A custom Android app built with MIT App Inventor. It connects to the ESP32 over Bluetooth Classic, presents four sliders (one per servo), and sends the corresponding angle command string on every slider change.

No third-party app needed — full control over the UI and command format.

Future Scope

With better hardware and further development, this project can evolve from a prototype into a real-world automation platform.

🎮 Gyro / Accelerometer Control

Replace the slider UI with a phone IMU or a standalone MPU-6050 worn on the hand. Map wrist orientation directly to arm joint angles for intuitive, gesture-based control.

🏭 Industrial Pick-and-Place

Scale up with higher-torque stepper or DC servo motors and a rigid metal chassis. Automate repetitive pick-and-place tasks on factory lines — from delicate CPU dies to heavier components.

🔧 Welding & Hazardous Tasks

Deploy in environments unsafe for humans — welding, chemical handling, or high-temperature assembly. Consistent, repeatable motion makes robotic arms ideal for precision industrial processes.

🤖 Autonomous Automation

Add computer vision (camera + ML model) to detect and classify objects. The arm can then autonomously sort, assemble, or inspect parts without human input — a step toward full factory automation.

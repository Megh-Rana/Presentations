What is a DAC?

The Problem

Computers process signals in discrete binary form — sequences of 0s and 1s. But the real world operates on continuous analog signals — sound, voltage, temperature.

The Solution

A Digital-to-Analog Converter (DAC) bridges this gap. It takes a binary digital input and converts it into a proportional analog voltage or current output.

⚡
Every speaker, display, and motor controller relies on DACs to interface digital systems with the physical world.

How DACs Work

The Conversion Process

A DAC takes an n-bit binary word and generates one of 2n possible voltage levels. The output steps through these levels as the digital input changes.

1
Receive the digital binary code
2
Weight each bit by its positional significance
3
Sum the weighted contributions
4
Output the resulting analog voltage

Resolution Matters

An 8-bit DAC produces 256 levels. A 16-bit DAC produces 65,536 levels — making the staircase output increasingly smooth and closer to the ideal analog signal.

Staircase Output — 3-bit DAC
Key Insight The gap between the staircase and the ideal line is the quantization error — the fundamental accuracy limitation of any DAC.

DAC Architectures

Three dominant architectures, each with different trade-offs between accuracy, speed, and complexity.

Binary Weighted

Uses resistors scaled in binary ratios: R, 2R, 4R, 8R…

Simple design Fast conversion Scales poorly Needs precise R values

R-2R Ladder

Uses only two resistor values (R and 2R) in a repeating ladder network.

Only 2 R values Scales well Matching still needed Moderate accuracy

Sigma-Delta (ΣΔ)

Uses oversampling and noise shaping to achieve high resolution digitally.

High resolution Excellent linearity High latency Complex design
04

Binary Weighted Resistor DAC

Working Principle

Each bit of the digital input controls a switch connected to a resistor. The resistor values are weighted in binary: the MSB has resistance R, the next has 2R, then 4R, and so on.

All switch outputs feed into a summing amplifier (op-amp), which adds the weighted currents to produce the analog output.

The Scaling Problem

For an 8-bit DAC, resistor values range from R to 128R. For 12 bits, that's R to 2048R. Manufacturing such a wide range with matched precision is extremely difficult and expensive.

This is why binary-weighted DACs are impractical beyond 4–6 bits in most real applications.

4-Bit Binary Weighted Circuit
Output Formula
Vout = −Vref · Rf · (B3/R + B2/2R + B1/4R + B0/8R)
05

R-2R Ladder DAC

Working Principle

Instead of binary-scaled resistors, the R-2R ladder uses only two resistor values: R and 2R, arranged in a repeating ladder network.

Each switch routes current either to the output summing node or to ground, based on the corresponding bit. The ladder network inherently creates the binary weighting through voltage division.

Why It's Better

Since only two resistor values are needed, matching is far easier even at high resolutions. A 16-bit R-2R DAC still only uses R and 2R — no extreme resistor ratios required.

This makes R-2R the most common architecture for precision and high-speed DACs in instrumentation and audio applications.

R-2R Ladder Network
Resistor types needed: Only 2 (R and 2R)
Scalability: Up to 16+ bits
Speed: Fast — no DSP overhead
Limitation: Resistor matching at high bits
06

Sigma-Delta (ΣΔ) DAC

A Different Approach

Unlike resistor-based DACs, Sigma-Delta converters achieve high accuracy through digital signal processing rather than precise analog components.

1
Oversampling — The input is sampled at many times the Nyquist rate (e.g., 64× or 256×)
2
Noise Shaping — Quantization noise is pushed to higher frequencies through feedback
3
Low-Pass Filtering — A filter removes the shaped noise, leaving a clean analog output

Key Advantage

Precision depends on time and digital math, not on matched resistors. This makes ΣΔ DACs the standard for high-resolution audio (24–32 bit) and low-cost consumer electronics.

Measuring Accuracy

Accuracy in DACs is not a single number — it's defined by multiple specifications that together determine how faithfully the output represents the digital input.

RES

Resolution

Number of bits the DAC can process. An n-bit DAC produces 2n discrete output levels.

8-bit → 256 levels 16-bit → 65,536 levels
DNL

Differential Non-Linearity

The deviation of each step size from the ideal 1 LSB. A DNL ≤ ±1 LSB guarantees monotonicity — the output never decreases as input increases.

Good: ±0.5 LSB Acceptable: ±1 LSB
INL

Integral Non-Linearity

The maximum deviation of the overall transfer curve from the ideal straight line. Represents the cumulative accuracy across the full range.

Precision: ±0.5 LSB General: ±1–2 LSB
ENOB

Effective Number of Bits

The real-world resolution after accounting for noise and distortion. A 16-bit DAC might only achieve 14 ENOB in practice.

Formula: ENOB = (SINAD − 1.76) / 6.02
08

Measuring Efficiency

Efficiency isn't just about power — it encompasses speed, area, and how much performance you get per watt.

PWR

Power Consumption

Total power drawn during conversion. Critical for battery-operated and IoT devices. Ranges from microwatts (low-power) to hundreds of milliwatts (high-speed).

DAC0808: ~33 mW AD9767: ~380 mW
SPD

Settling Time

Time for output to reach and stay within ±0.5 LSB of the final value. Faster settling = higher throughput.

High-Speed: 1–100 ns Precision: 1–50 μs
FOM

Figure of Merit

Normalizes performance across designs. Walden FoM = Power / (2ENOB × fs). Lower is better — shows energy per conversion step.

State-of-art: <10 fJ/conv
AREA

Silicon Area

Die area consumed by the DAC circuit. ΣΔ DACs use less analog area but more digital. R-2R needs matched resistor arrays that consume more space.

Trade-off: Analog vs Digital area

The Accuracy vs Efficiency Trade-off

No single architecture wins everywhere. The choice depends on whether your application prioritizes precision, speed, or low power.

Resolution 4–8 bits 8–16 bits 16–32 bits

Real-World Applications

Audio Systems

ΣΔ DACs dominate — smartphones, headphone amps, and studio gear use 24–32 bit converters for high-fidelity playback.

Example: PCM1794 (24-bit, 192 kHz)

Telecommunications

High-speed R-2R DACs generate waveforms for 4G/5G base stations, radar, and Software Defined Radio (SDR).

Example: AD9767 (14-bit, 125 MSPS)

Industrial Control

R-2R or string DACs drive precision voltage outputs for motor controllers, PLCs, and calibration instruments.

Monotonicity guaranteed (DNL ≤ ±1 LSB)

Medical Devices

High-resolution DACs in imaging systems (MRI, CT) and waveform generators for patient monitoring and ultrasound.

Requires both precision + low noise
DAC0808 8-bit · ~33 mW · 150 ns settling
AD9767 14-bit · 380 mW · 125 MSPS
PCM1794 24-bit · 132 dB DNR · 192 kHz
11

Summary & Key Takeaways

1
No perfect DAC exists — every architecture trades off accuracy against speed, power, and cost.
2
Binary Weighted DACs are simple and fast but impractical beyond low resolutions due to resistor matching.
3
R-2R Ladder DACs offer the best balance — practical to manufacture, scalable, and fast. The workhorse of precision and high-speed applications.
4
Sigma-Delta DACs achieve the highest resolution through digital processing, ideal for audio and low-cost consumer electronics.
5
Application requirements drive the choice: prioritize speed → R-2R, prioritize accuracy → ΣΔ, need simplicity → Binary Weighted.
